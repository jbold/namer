# Strategic Naming Framework

The Diamond Framework — developed by David Placek at Lexicon Branding (Swiffer, Sonos, Azure, Vercel, Pentium, BlackBerry, Impossible Foods) — forces clarity on what the name must do before you generate a single candidate.

## Step 0: What's the domain?

Ask this first. It sets context for everything else.

> "What industry or category is this? (e.g. coffee shop, developer tool, law firm, band)"

**Why first:** The domain determines what seeds to generate, what sounds feel right, and what to search for in availability checks. "Atlas" is clear for a coffee shop, taken for software.

## The Diamond

```
     What does winning
        look like?
          (top)
         /      \
   What do       What do
   we need       we have
   to say?       to win?
   (left)        (right)
         \      /
       What do we
       need to win?
        (bottom)
```

### 1. What does winning look like?

The vision. What is this thing, who's it for, and what does success look like?

> **Swiffer example:** "Build a mop-like device that people will pay a premium price for. Make cleaning floors something people actually want to do."
>
> **Vercel example:** "Be the most innovative, fastest-moving company in the frontend deployment category."

*Seeds to extract:* Words that capture the ambition — premium, fast, innovative, joyful, dominant.

### 2. What do we have to win?

The advantage. What assets, insights, or capabilities give you an edge?

> **Swiffer example:** "P&G's Pampers diaper technology — a streamlined, lighter, more effective cleaning tool that uses absorbent pads instead of water and a mop head."
>
> **Vercel example:** "Creator of Next.js, deep developer community, speed-obsessed engineering culture."

*Seeds to extract:* Words that capture differentiators — streamlined, light, absorbent, swift, precision, craft.

### 3. What do we need to win?

The gaps. What's missing? What must be overcome?

> **Swiffer example:** "Avoid being seen as just another mop. People hate mopping — we need them to see this as something entirely new."
>
> **Vercel example:** "Break out of the developer-only perception. Attract enterprise buyers, not just indie hackers."

*Seeds to extract:* Words that counter the gaps — fresh, new, break, beyond, transform.

### 4. What do we need to say?

The message. What should the name *communicate*? What should it *feel* like?

> **Swiffer example:** "Two messages: logical (efficient, quick, easy) + emotional (fun, joyful, light). The name should sound like a quick, satisfying action."
>
> **Vercel example:** "Speed, flow, precision, alive. The name should sound daring and energetic."

*Seeds to extract:* Feeling words — swift, snap, breeze, play, flow, alive, dare, spark.

## Seed Extraction

After all 5 questions, extract **15-25 seed words** total:
- ~3-5 from domain (the industry's vocabulary)
- ~3-5 from "winning" (the vision)
- ~3-5 from "have to win" (the advantages)
- ~2-3 from "need to win" (the gaps/aspirations)
- ~3-5 from "need to say" (the feeling/tone)

These seeds feed directly into `generate.py --seeds "word1,word2,..."`. The quality of your seeds determines the quality of your candidates.

## Sound Symbolism

Research shows certain phonemes carry consistent psychological associations. Use the "need to say" answer to pick the right sounds:

| Sound | Effect | Example |
|-------|--------|---------|
| V | Alive, vibrant, energetic | **V**ercel |
| B | Reliable, solid, grounded | **B**lackBerry |
| Z | Attention-drawing, electric | A**z**ure |
| X | Innovative, edgy, technical | Ne**x**t |
| Sw- | Swift, clean, satisfying action | **Sw**iffer |
| -ium | Scientific, fundamental, elemental | Pent**ium** |
| Short i | Light, slim, quick | Sw**i**ffer, Dasan**i** |

> "Imagine the sound of your brand before you start naming. Should your name sound fast? Full? Reliable?" — David Placek

## Key Principles

1. **Generate at least 1,000 candidates** before evaluating. Lexicon usually generates thousands. Shortlist to 50-100.
2. **Separate creation from judgment**: Generate (no filter) → discuss ("what could this do?") → prune rigorously.
3. **Invented names take LESS money to build** than existing words — they signal "new and innovative" and generate more attention. Humans are drawn to the new.
4. **Great names make you uncomfortable**: If it feels safe, it's probably wrong.
5. **Compounds are multipliers**: Two words combined create 1+1=3 mental associations.
6. **The best names don't describe reality; they change it.** Swiffer made mopping feel like a quick, joyful action. Impossible made plant-based feel daring.
7. **Competitor test**: Don't ask "what do you think?" — ask "your competitor just launched with this name — reaction?"
8. **Don't brainstorm with 4+ people** — peer pressure kills originality. Small teams or solo exploration, then merge.
